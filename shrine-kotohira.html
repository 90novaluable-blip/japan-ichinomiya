Ōmononushi-no-Kami
大物主神 — also enshrines Emperor Sutoku (崇徳天皇)

Ōmononushi-no-Kami has been worshipped since antiquity as the supreme guardian of the seas. Sailors, fishermen, and merchants across Japan have placed their faith in this deity for maritime safety and prosperous voyages. As the head shrine of approximately 600 Kotohira shrines nationwide, Kotohira-gu stands as the spiritual anchor for all who depend upon the ocean.

Essence

The Mountain Shrine of the Sea God

Perched on the slopes of sacred Mt. Zōzu at 421 meters above sea level, Kotohira-gu presents a beautiful paradox: Japan's most revered sea deity resides not beside the ocean, but high upon a mountain. For over a thousand years, pilgrims have climbed 1,368 stone steps to reach this sanctuary — a journey of devotion once considered one of the great pilgrimages of a lifetime.
During the Edo period (1603–1868), ordinary citizens were generally forbidden from traveling freely, but an exception was made for religious pilgrimages to shrines like Ise Jingū and Kotohira-gu. "Konpira-mairi" (pilgrimage to Konpira) became enormously popular, with hundreds of thousands making the journey each year. So powerful was the desire to visit that people who couldn't travel themselves would tie a purse of coins around the neck of their dog and send the animal on the pilgrimage — an extraordinary custom known as "Konpira-inu" (Konpira dog). The faithful canine would be passed from traveler to traveler until it reached the shrine, where priests would offer prayers on behalf of the absent owner.

Access

Your Journey to Kotohira-gu

  • 🚂
    By Train (Recommended)
    From Takamatsu: Kotoden Kotohira Line from Takamatsu-Chikko Sta. → Kotoden Kotohira Sta. (~60 min). From Okayama: JR Seto-Ōhashi Line + Dosan Line to JR Kotohira Sta. (~75 min). From Tokyo: Shinkansen to Okayama, then transfer (~4 hrs total).
  • 🚗
    By Car
    From Zentsuji IC (Takamatsu Expressway): ~15 min. Multiple paid parking lots available near the shrine approach (around ¥300–500). No official shrine parking.
  • 🚶
    Walking from Station
    From JR Kotohira Sta.: ~15 min walk to step 1. From Kotoden Kotohira Sta.: ~8 min walk to step 1. From step 1 to main shrine (785 steps): ~30–45 min. From main shrine to inner shrine (583 more steps): ~30 min.
  • ♿
    Accessibility
    The shrine approach involves steep stone steps. A kago (palanquin) service was historically available but has been discontinued. The lower sections of the approach have a gentler slope. Those with mobility challenges may find the climb very difficult.
  • 🕐
    Hours
    Shrine grounds: 6:00–18:00 (Apr–Sep), 6:00–17:00 (Oct–Mar). Treasure House: 8:30–17:00 (¥800). Shoin Hall: 8:30–17:00 (¥800). The inner shrine (Okusha) goshuin office closes at 16:30.
Deep Insight

What Makes Kotohira-gu Truly Special

Unique
The Konpira Dog — A Pilgrimage by Proxy
In the Edo period, people who couldn't make the journey would send their dogs in their place. Coins were tied around the dog's neck, and travelers along the road would pass the animal forward until it reached the shrine. A bronze statue of the Konpira-inu stands near the main shrine — look for it after step 628.
Art
Maruyama Ōkyo's Tigers in the Omote Shoin
The Omote Shoin (front reception hall), an Important Cultural Property, houses stunning fusuma paintings by Maruyama Ōkyo (1733–1795), one of Japan's most celebrated painters. His "Tigers Drinking Water" depicts tigers so lifelike they seem ready to leap from the sliding doors. The Shoin also features landscape paintings and works by Itō Jakuchũ.
Unique
Kamiyo Ame — The 1,300-Year-Old Candy
On the approach between steps 365 and 431, you'll encounter the "Five Families" (Gonin Byakushō) — the only merchants historically permitted to sell within the shrine precincts. Under large white parasols, they sell kamiyo ame, golden fan-shaped candy that comes with a tiny hammer. You break it into pieces and share — spreading the blessings of Konpira.
Tip
Go to the Okusha (Inner Shrine) — Most Visitors Don't
Most visitors stop at the main shrine (step 785), but the truly rewarding experience is climbing all 1,368 steps to the inner shrine (Izutama Jinja). The path becomes a serene forest trail, the crowds thin out, and the view from the top is breathtaking. The goshuin at the Okusha is different from the main shrine — a special memento for those who make the full climb.
Seasonal
Konpira Stone Steps Marathon (October)
Every October, a unique marathon is held on the 785 stone steps to the main shrine. Runners race up and down the stairs in an exhilarating test of endurance and agility. It's an incredible spectacle and a wonderful time to experience the energy and community spirit of Kotohira.
Eat / Stay / More

Around Kotohira-gu

🍽️
Sanuki Udon Paradise
Kagawa is the undisputed capital of udon. The approach to Kotohira-gu is lined with udon shops serving fresh, hand-made noodles. Try shōyu udon (soy sauce udon) — a Kagawa specialty where thick, chewy noodles are eaten with just soy sauce and a raw egg. Also try "Kyūman" (灸まん), a moxibustion-shaped sweet bun that's been a Konpira souvenir for generations.
🏨
Konpira Onsen — Hot Spring Ryokan
Kotohira offers excellent hot spring ryokan for post-climb relaxation. Kotohira Kadan is a 400-year-old inn once frequented by literary giants like Mori Ōgai. Kotosankaku offers modern comfort with rooftop baths. Shikishimakan, housed in a restored registered cultural property, features four private baths of cypress, rock, ceramic, and bamboo.
🗺️
Kanamaru-za & Beyond
Visit Kanamaru-za (旧金毘羅大芝居), Japan's oldest surviving kabuki theater, built in 1835 and designated an Important Cultural Property. Every spring, professional kabuki performances are held here — a truly rare experience. Also explore the Maritime Museum (琴平海洋博物館) celebrating Konpira's connection to the sea, and nearby Zentsu-ji Temple, birthplace of Kūkai (Kōbō Daishi).

The approach is lined with souvenir shops, teahouses, and historic buildings. Key landmarks: Step 365 — the Five Families candy sellers; Step 431 — the great stone lantern; Step 628 — Asahi-sha shrine (Important Cultural Property); Step 785 — the main shrine with its panoramic view of the Sanuki Plain. Beyond lies the forest path to the inner shrine — increasingly quiet, deeply spiritual.
