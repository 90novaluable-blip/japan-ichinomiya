Atsuta-no-Okami
熱田大神

Atsuta-no-Okami is the sacred manifestation of Amaterasu Ōmikami — the Sun Goddess — as she dwells within the Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi, the legendary sword that is one of Japan's three Imperial Regalia. Also enshrined alongside are four great deities: Susanoo-no-Mikoto, Yamato Takeru, Miyazu-hime, and Take Inadane-no-Mikoto — all bound together by the mythic journey of the sacred blade. Atsuta Jingu is second in national importance only to Ise, revered by emperors, samurai generals, and ordinary worshippers for over 1,900 years.

1 Essence
Why this shrine commands awe

In the heart of Japan's third-largest city, a 190,000 m² ancient forest grows undisturbed. Inside it, sealed away from any human eye, rests the Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi — the Grass-Cutting Sword, one of three objects that legitimate the Japanese imperial throne. It has never been displayed, never been photographed, and its very appearance is unknown. Atsuta Jingu exists, in part, simply to guard this secret.

Founded in 113 CE during Emperor Keiko's reign, Atsuta Jingu has stood for over 1,900 years — making it one of Japan's oldest still-active shrines. Oda Nobunaga prayed here the night before the Battle of Okehazama (1560), where his vastly outnumbered army destroyed Imagawa Yoshimoto. In gratitude, he donated the magnificent stone-walled enclosure that still bears his name: the Nobunaga-bei. Throughout Japanese history, whoever sought to rule Japan first sought the blessing of Atsuta's sword.

3 Deep Insight
What repeat visitors discover
UNIQUE
The Sword No One Has Ever Seen The Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi is sealed inside the innermost sanctuary and has never been shown to any person — not even the shrine priests. Its physical appearance is completely unknown. This absolute mystery, maintained for nearly two millennia, gives Atsuta an atmosphere unlike any other shrine in Japan.
UNIQUE
450 Swords in the Kusanagi-kan The Treasure House contains over 450 historical swords — donated by emperors, shoguns, and samurai lords across 1,900 years. The collection is rotated monthly. Sword enthusiasts come specifically to see masterworks by legendary smiths on display, making this the finest sword museum in Japan attached to a living shrine.
INFO
Nobunaga's Stone Wall, Still Standing After Oda Nobunaga's miraculous victory at Okehazama (1560), he donated a 100-meter stone retaining wall to Atsuta as thanks. The Nobunaga-bei still stands along the shrine's southern perimeter — a direct, touchable connection to one of Japan's most dramatic military turning points.
INFO
The 1,000-Year-Old Camphor Tree The shrine's most famous tree — a colossal camphor (kusu) — is said to have been planted by Kōbō Daishi (Kukai), the founder of Shingon Buddhism, over 1,000 years ago. Its roots spread across the forest floor like a living cathedral. Local tradition holds that you can feel its spiritual energy simply by placing your hands on the bark.
SEASONAL
Atsuta Festival (June 5) The annual Atsuta Matsuri (Shōbu Festival) draws over 60,000 visitors in a single night. The grounds fill with lanterns, and devotees can witness kyudo archery, bugaku court dance, kendo, and sumo — all dedicated as offerings to the shrine. The evening processions through the camphor forest are breathtaking.
4 Eat · Stay · More
Making the most of your Nagoya visit
🍜
Miya Kishimen

Founded in 1923 and still located inside the shrine grounds, Miya Kishimen serves Nagoya's famous flat noodles (kishimen) in rich dashi broth. Eating here — in a teahouse within the sacred precincts — is a ritual unto itself. Try the kake kishimen with a side of miso katsu.

🍡
Kiyome Mochi

Since the Edo period, the Kiyome Chaya teahouse has sold these delicate white rice cakes filled with smooth red bean paste. Pilgrims have eaten them for centuries. The shop near the south entrance lets you watch them being made fresh. The perfect shrine souvenir.

🗺️
Nagoya Castle & Beyond

Nagoya Castle (30 min by subway) is one of Japan's grandest castle complexes with golden shachihoko fish on the roof. The Noritake Garden (ceramics history) and Osu Kannon shopping district round out a full Nagoya day. Both Ise Jingu and Inuyama Castle are day-trip distance.

草薙神剣 · Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi
The Mythic Journey of Japan's Most Sacred Sword — 1,900 Years of Guardianship
🐍
Born from a Dragon's Tail

In the age of the gods, Susanoo-no-Mikoto slew the eight-headed serpent Yamata-no-Orochi. From within the creature's fourth tail, he drew a brilliant sword unlike any other. He named it Ame-no-Murakumo-no-Tsurugi — "Sword of the Gathering Clouds of Heaven" — and offered it to his sister Amaterasu.

🔥
Yamato Takeru Cuts Through Fire

Amaterasu gave the sword to her grandson Ninigi, from whom it passed to Prince Yamato Takeru. Lured into a burning field by enemies, Yamato Takeru used the sword to cut the surrounding grass — and the wind turned the fire back on his attackers. From that day, the sword was renamed Kusanagi-no-Tsurugi: "Grass-Cutter."

⛩️
A Shrine Built to Guard It

After Yamato Takeru's death, his wife Miyazu-hime enshrined the sword at Atsuta — the site of their home. Emperor Keiko commanded that a formal shrine be built to honor and protect it. That shrine, founded in 113 CE, is Atsuta Jingu. For nearly two thousand years, the shrine has kept the sword sealed, unseen, and protected.

👑
Symbol of Imperial Legitimacy

Together with the Yata-no-Kagami mirror (at Ise) and the Yasakani-no-Magatama jewel, Kusanagi forms the Three Imperial Regalia (三種の神器). These three objects — passed from emperor to emperor for millennia — are the ultimate symbols of the Japanese imperial throne. Atsuta guards one of the three.
