Enshrined Deity
Takemikazuchi no Okami
武甕槌大神 — God of Thunder, Lightning & Martial Valor
Born from the blood of a slain fire deity, Takemikazuchi descended to the land of Izumo and — with a single display of his divine sword — pacified all gods who resisted the rule of Heaven. Warriors, generals, and samurai throughout history have made the long pilgrimage to Kashima to seek his blessing before battle. Fujiwara clan, Minamoto no Yoritomo, Tokugawa Ieyasu — all came here. His power governs thunder, victory, protection, and the forging of fate.

由緒 · Why It Matters
神宮の名を持つ、三社のひとつ。
Until the Meiji era, only three shrines in all of Japan bore the supreme title "Jingū" — Ise, Katori, and Kashima. Founded (according to tradition) in 660 BC, Kashima Jingū stands apart as the shrine where Japan's very destiny was shaped: its deity personally pacified the land before the first emperor could rule.
深読み · The Deeper Story
The Kashima Shinto-ryu, founded here in the 15th century, is widely regarded as Japan's oldest systematized martial art. Its techniques were the basis for every major sword school that followed — kendo, iaido, ninjutsu. Even today, Japanese athletes in kendo, judo, and other martial sports travel to Kashima before major competitions. The shrine also holds a National Treasure: a straight sword (chokuto) over 2.7 meters long — a blade forged for a god.

Insider's Guide · 深掘り リピーターのこだわり — 5つの必見ポイント 深度体验 · 行家才知道的5件事
UNIQUE
Sacred Deer — Messengers of the God
The deer of Kashima are considered divine messengers (shinkoku). Legend holds that when the god Takemikazuchi traveled to Nara, his deer accompanied him — which is why Nara's famous deer are considered descendants of Kashima's sacred animals. About 60 deer still roam the inner deer park today.
MYSTERY
Kaname-ishi — The Earthquake Keystone
Buried deep in the earth near the inner shrine is a stone called Kaname-ishi (要石, "keystone"). Only a small tip is visible. Myth holds it pins down the head of the giant underground catfish (namazu) whose thrashing causes earthquakes. A similar stone exists at Katori Shrine nearby.
UNIQUE
Mitarashi Pond — The Spring That Never Overflows
This crystal-clear spring produces an estimated 400,000 liters of fresh water daily — yet the pond has never overflowed and never dried in recorded history. In January, over 200 people participate in the Great Cold Purification Ritual (Kanmisogi) by immersing themselves in its icy waters.
TIP
Walk the Oku-Sando — The Inner Forest Path
Beyond the main hall, a 500-meter moss-covered stone path leads through a 70-hectare ancient forest to the Oku-miya (inner shrine), donated by Tokugawa Ieyasu in 1605. The forest holds over 800 plant species and is a prefectural Natural Monument. Go early morning for complete silence.
FESTIVAL
Ofune Matsuri — Once Every 12 Years
The legendary Ship Festival (Ofune Matsuri) has been held since ancient times and occurs only once every 12 years, drawing pilgrims from across Japan. Over 80 annual festivals take place throughout the year, including spectacular autumn festivals and New Year celebrations that draw nearly 600,000 visitors in three days.

The shrine approach is lined with stalls serving hamaguri (clam) dishes — hamaguri curry, hamaguri ramen — alongside namazu (catfish) hot pot at century-old restaurants, and anko (anglerfish) stew in winter.

Chiba Prefecture · Deity of the Sword
In the ancient Shinto hierarchy, the title "Jingū" (神宮) was reserved for the most sacred shrines of all — those whose deities directly shaped the fate of Japan. For over a thousand years, this honor was held by only three: Ise, enshrining the Sun Goddess Amaterasu; Kashima, enshrining the god who pacified the land; and Katori, enshrining the god who aided that pacification. All other shrines, however grand, were called "Taisha" or "Jinja." Today's pilgrims who walk the Eastern Jingū circuit in a single day are following a tradition that stretches back over a thousand years.
🪨 The Kaname-ishi: Japan's Earthquake Suppressor
Deep beneath the grounds of Kashima Jingū lies the Kaname-ishi (要石) — the "keystone of the earth." Only a small rounded tip, about the size of a fist, protrudes above the soil. According to Shinto mythology, the stone penetrates into the underworld and pins down the enormous catfish (Ōnamazu) whose writhing body causes earthquakes across Japan. An identical stone exists at Katori Jingū 30 km away — and together, the two stones are said to secure both ends of the creature. Pilgrims touch the stone and pray for protection from disasters. Scientists in the Edo period reportedly tried to dig up the stone for three consecutive days and nights — and found it had no bottom.
