Enshrined Deities
Takeminakata & Yasakatome
建御名方神 · 八坂刀売神
Takeminakata-no-kami is the son of Ōkuninushi, the great creator god of Izumo. After losing a divine contest of strength to the warrior god Takemikazuchi, he fled to Suwa and vowed never to leave — and so the deity is believed to literally reside here still, bound to this sacred land. He rules over wind, water, farming, hunting, and martial arts, and was venerated by Japan's greatest samurai clans. Beside him, Yasakatome-no-kami is his consort, enshrined at the Upper Shrine's Maemiya.

由緒 · Why It Matters
本殿を持たない、山と木が神体。
One of Japan's oldest shrines, cited in the 8th-century Kojiki, Suwa Taisha consists of four separate shrines around Lake Suwa — and three of them have no honden (main hall) at all. The deity's body is the mountain, the sacred trees, and the land itself.
A shrine older than the written record. When Empress Suiko's court compiled the Kojiki in 712 CE, Suwa Taisha was already ancient. Takeda Shingen, the great warlord of Kai, prayed here before every campaign and credited his victories to the martial god's blessing. The Hōjō clan, too, bore the shrine's symbol. For over a millennium, no warrior dared face battle without Suwa's blessing.

Deep Insight · 深掘り リピーターのこだわり — 知る人ぞ知る 深度探索 · 行家才知道的
Unique
No main hall — the mountain IS the deity
At Kamisha Honmiya, there is no honden (inner hall). You pray directly toward sacred Mt. Moriya (守屋山). At the Lower Shrine, the go-shintai are living trees: a thousand-year-old sugi at Harumiya and a yew at Akimiya. Nature itself is the god.
Festival
Onbashira — Japan's most dangerous festival (every 6 years)
Sixteen giant fir trees (each ~10 tons, 17m long) are felled in the mountains, then dragged by thousands of men and ridden down a 30° slope to the four shrines. Held every 6 years in years of the Tiger and Monkey. Next: 2028.
Ritual
Collect all four goshuin — and earn a special gift
Each of the four shrines offers its own unique goshuin (shrine seal). Collect stamps from all four and present them at the final shrine office to receive a commemorative certificate. Allow a full day with a car.
History
Takeda Shingen's sacred sanctuary
The great warlord Takeda Shingen of Kai Province was a fervent devotee of Suwa. He prayed here before every military campaign and took the shrine's sacred banner into battle. Suwa Taisha was effectively the spiritual headquarters of medieval Japanese warfare.
Tip
Best route: Maemiya → Honmiya (Upper), then Harumiya → Akimiya (Lower)
Start at the Upper Shrine (Maemiya first — the spiritual origin), then head north around the lake to the Lower Shrine. The drive along Lake Suwa's shore is scenic. End at Akimiya for the grand Kaguraden (music hall) and the 1-ton sacred rope.

Eat · Stay · Explore 周辺の滞在 — 食・泊・次の旅へ 周边吃住行 · 延伸旅程
Local Sake 🍶
Kamisuwa Sake Alley
Five sake breweries line up in just 400 meters near Kami-Suwa Station. Suwa's cold mountain water and crisp air produce some of Japan's finest sake — many offer free tastings. Don't miss Masumi (真澄), founded 1662.
Local Food 🍜
Soba & Unagi
Nagano is Japan's soba capital. Around Lake Suwa, also try freshwater eel (unagi) from the lake — a regional speciality for centuries. Chabo Shokudo near Kami-Suwa Station serves a beloved massive chicken katsu.
Onsen Stay ♨️
Lake Suwa Onsen Hotels
The Suwa lakeside has excellent hot spring hotels with views of the water. In winter, the lake sometimes freezes and creates "Omiwatari" (御神渡り) — sacred ice ridges believed to be the gods' crossing. Plan a winter visit for this rare sight.
Nature 🏔️
Mt. Kirigamine & Tateshina
The highland plateau of Kirigamine (霧ヶ峰) offers summer wildflower hiking and winter skiing — 30 min by car from the shrines. Tateshina's birch forests and Shirakabako lake are stunning in autumn foliage.
Nearby Sight 🏯
Takato Castle Ruins
1 hr north, Takato Castle (高遠城) is famous as one of Japan's top 3 cherry blossom spots. Over 1,500 trees turn deep pink in April — an otherworldly sight against the Ina Valley mountains.
Next Ichinomiya ⛩️
Mino Ichinomiya: Nangū Taisha
Head southwest ~2.5 hrs to Nangū Taisha in Gifu — the ichinomiya of Mino Province, enshrining the god of metalworking. Or head north to 気多大社 in Ishikawa (4 hrs) to continue your Tōsandō pilgrimage.

Once every six years, in years corresponding to the Tiger (寅) and Monkey (申) on the zodiac cycle, Suwa Taisha undergoes its most spectacular ritual: the Onbashira Matsuri (御柱祭).

Sixteen enormous fir trees are selected from the sacred forests of Mt. Yatsugatake, each measuring about 17 meters in length and weighing up to 10 tons. Thousands of men haul each log down the mountain using ropes — then, in the most dramatic moment, volunteers ride the logs as they slide down a 30° slope called Kiotoshi (木落し). Injuries are common; it is said that dying in this act is considered an honor.

The logs are then erected at the four corners of each shrine as sacred boundary markers — replacing the previous set that has stood for six years. The ritual has been performed continuously for over 1,200 years, and is one of the largest festivals in Japan. As many as 200,000 spectators gather to watch.

The word Onbashira (御柱) means "the honored pillar." Through this festival, the shrines are symbolically renewed and the deity's power refreshed for another cycle of six years.
