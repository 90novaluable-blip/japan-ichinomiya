The Essence
Why Izumo Taisha matters

Japan's oldest and most august shrine — where myths live and the gods themselves come to rest.

Izumo Taisha predates written history. Already venerated when Japan's oldest chronicles (the Kojiki, 712 CE) were compiled, the shrine is dedicated to Okuninushi, who built Japan's land and civilisation before ceding rule to the heavenly deities. As a reward, he was enshrined here in a palace that, according to legend, once soared 96 metres into the sky. Today's towering 24-metre Honden, designated a National Treasure in 1952, still commands awe — the tallest shrine hall in Japan.

Getting There
Izumo, Shimane Prefecture
  • ✈️By Air: Fly Haneda → Izumo Airport (~1 hr 25 min), then bus direct to shrine (~30 min). Fastest option from Tokyo.
  • 🚂By Train from Tokyo: Shinkansen to Okayama (~3.5 hr), then Limited Express Yakumo to Izumo-shi (~2 hr), then Ichibata Railway to Izumotaisha-mae (~45 min). Total ~6 hrs.
  • 🚂By Train from Osaka: Shinkansen to Okayama (~55 min), then Limited Express Yakumo to Izumo-shi (~2 hr), then Ichibata Railway. Total ~3.5 hrs.
  • 🚗By Car: ~6 hrs from Tokyo on expressway. Parking near the shrine approach is free (spacious lots).
  • 🕐Hours: 6:00–19:00 daily (main approach & Haiden). Inner Hon-den precincts: 6:00–16:30. Free admission.
  • ♿Accessibility: Mostly flat gravel paths. Wheelchair accessible routes available via the side entrance.

Deep Insight
5 things only regulars know
UNIQUE
Four Claps, Not Two. At Izumo Taisha, the correct form of prayer (Shime no Yotsude) is 2 bows, 4 claps, 1 bow — not the standard 2 claps used at other shrines. Four claps honour the four deities enshrined in the corridor halls alongside the main deity.
UNIQUE
The Reversed Shimenawa. The giant sacred rope at the Kaguraden (13.5 m long, 5 tons) hangs in reverse orientation — thick end on the left. This is unique to Izumo Taisha and reflects an ancient, independent Shinto tradition predating standardised practice.
INFO
The Invisible Honden. The main hall (Honden) itself cannot be viewed by visitors. Only the outer corridors are accessible. Okuninushi faces west — toward the unseen world — not south like most enshrined deities, making this a uniquely other-worldly presence.
SEASONAL
Kamiari-zuki: The Month the Gods Arrive. During the 10th lunar month, 8 million gods from across Japan travel to Izumo for a divine conference on human fates and relationships. While the rest of Japan calls it 'Kannazuki' (month without gods), Izumo celebrates 'Kamiari-zuki' (month WITH gods). The Kamiari Festival runs for 8 days.
TIP
The Lucky White Rabbits. Dozens of small rabbit statues dot the shrine grounds, referencing the myth of the White Rabbit of Inaba — a story of Okuninushi's compassion. Look for them in different poses along the approach; finding one with the same zodiac animal as you is said to bring especially strong blessings.

Eat · Stay · More
Around Izumo
🍜
Izumo Soba
Japan's finest buckwheat noodles, served in three stacked lacquer bowls (warigo). The nutty, dark soba is ground with the husk for depth of flavour. Dozens of shops line the approach.
🏨
Kamiari no Yado Okamoto
A traditional inn steps from the shrine, offering kaiseki cuisine with local Shimane ingredients. Ideal for a dawn visit before crowds arrive.
🗺️
Inasa no Hama Beach
According to mythology, this is where 8 million gods arrive by sea each year. A sacred rock (Benzaiten) sits offshore. Beautiful at sunset. 15-min walk from the shrine.
